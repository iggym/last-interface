field notes on the agentic economy

Your agent is on hold.
The question is who gets paid for the wait.

Consumer AI agents now cancel your gym membership, argue with Comcast, and file the refund claim you kept meaning to. That part's real. What's less obvious is what happens once the company on the other end of the line has an agent too.

Your call is important to us… 00:00

Here's the pitch you've heard: an agent sits on hold so you don't have to, argues with a retention rep, and gets you a better deal. Companies built entire products on it — Pine, Rocket Money, DoNotPay, Kudos — and the results are not nothing: one vendor's own data shows users saving hundreds of dollars a year canceling a single forgotten cable add-on.

But almost every write-up stops at "does the agent win the call." That's the wrong question to be asking by 2026. Below are six small tools built around the numbers behind this industry — and the reframe that the people building these agents actually operate on, which has very little to do with how persuasive the AI sounds on the phone.

The Cut

Named because it's the first fact that gets buried under "we saved you money" marketing. Most negotiation agents don't charge you a subscription — they take a slice of the savings, typically 35–60% in the first year. The agent's win and your win are correlated but not identical. Every tool below eventually runs into The Cut in some form.

Hold Music Arbitrage

The honest version of the value prop isn't "the agent negotiates better than you." It's simpler: your time has a price, hold music doesn't, and an agent can sit in the queue at $0/hour while you do something else. That's the actual arbitrage. The question is whether the fee it charges is smaller than the value of the time it bought back.

Bot vs. Bot

The mental shift practitioners in this space have already made, and most newcomers haven't: this isn't a story about whether AI can out-negotiate a human rep. Retention teams are building their own agents — scoring login frequency, feature use, and billing timing to flag you as a churn risk and intervene before you cancel. The fight increasingly happens between two systems, at machine speed, with the human mostly watching the outcome.

The Cut, revisited

DoNotPay's problem wasn't that its agent failed sometimes — every agent does. It's that the company sold certainty ("substitute for a human lawyer") it hadn't tested, while quietly keeping The Cut on outcomes it couldn't actually guarantee. The FTC's $193,000 order didn't punish automation. It punished claiming more capability than the agent had earned.

The reframe, stated plainly

A smart newcomer asks: "is the agent good enough to win the call?" That's the wrong constraint. Practitioners in this space are increasingly asking a different question: once both sides of a transaction have an agent, who captures the value the negotiation creates — the platform taking The Cut, the company's retention system playing Bot vs. Bot, or you? Capability was the bottleneck in 2023. By 2026 the bottleneck is closer to market structure: fee splits, data access, and which side's agent has the better model of the other.
